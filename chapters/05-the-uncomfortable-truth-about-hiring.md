# Chapter 5: The Uncomfortable Truth About Hiring

Most startups hire to solve problems they haven't diagnosed.

The product isn't growing fast enough. So they hire a growth person. The code is buggy. So they hire another engineer. The customers keep complaining. So they hire someone for support. The revenue isn't where it should be. So they hire a salesperson.

None of these are wrong, exactly. But they're all second-order responses to first-order problems.

Why isn't the product growing? Usually not because there aren't enough people working on growth. Usually because the product isn't good enough yet, or the market fit is wrong, or the positioning is off. Hiring a growth person to spray traffic at a leaky bucket just increases the water bill.

Why is the code buggy? Usually not because there aren't enough engineers. Usually because the architecture was rushed, or the testing culture is poor, or the senior engineer who understood the tradeoffs left six months ago and took the context with them. Adding another engineer without fixing those things just means more bugs written faster.

Here's a third version I've watched happen. A company's churn is too high. So they hire a customer success manager. The CS manager runs onboarding calls and sends check-in emails and builds a whole little program. Churn stays high. Because the problem wasn't onboarding. The problem was that the product had a core workflow nobody could figure out without hand-holding. The CS manager was professional hand-holding at scale. The company hired around a product problem and called it fixed. They hired a second CS manager six months later.

Hiring is how startups avoid doing the hard thinking.

---

I'm going to say something that will make some people uncomfortable: most early-stage startups hire for emotional reasons.

Not financial reasons. Emotional ones.

There's the **loneliness hire**. The founder who's been working solo for a year and needs someone to talk to. So they hire a "co-founder" or a "head of product" who's really just a companion. The title sounds strategic. The function is social.

There's the **credibility hire**. The startup that brings on a notable name to put in the pitch deck, even though that person will never do meaningful work. The hire isn't for the company. It's for the slide.

There's the **hope hire**. The sales leader who's going to "unlock the enterprise market" based on a resume that describes a very different company at a very different stage. The founder knows, somewhere, that this person can't close deals that the product can't support. But hope is a powerful drug and enterprise feels like a plan.

There's the **anxiety hire**. The founder who's terrified of a specific risk, so they hire someone to manage that risk, even though what they actually needed was to fix the thing that created the risk. Hiring a head of security because the product has security holes. Hiring a compliance person because the processes are a mess. The problems stay. They just have someone new to report on them.

None of these are malicious. They're human. Building a company is terrifying and exhausting and isolating, and hiring feels like progress. The headcount goes up, the bank balance goes down, and for a few weeks, the founder feels like they're doing something.

Then the new hire needs onboarding. And context. And management. And suddenly the founder is spending twenty hours a week supporting the people they hired to take work off their plate.

---

The uncomfortable truth: hiring is often an admission that you haven't figured out the problem yet.

When you're forced to solve problems with fewer people — because you can't afford to hire, or because you've decided not to hire, or because you're running the experiment I've been running for years — you solve them differently.

You automate. You simplify. You eliminate.

You ask: does this task actually need to happen? If it does, does a human need to do it? If a human needs to do it, does it need to happen now?

Most tasks fail at least one of these tests. Most of the work that companies hire people to do is work the company invented for itself.

Customer support tickets that wouldn't exist if the product were clearer. Meeting notes that exist because the decisions aren't being made clearly. Status updates that exist because nobody trusts the system to surface what needs attention. Process documentation that exists because the processes are too complicated for anyone to remember.

Fix the product. Fix the decisions. Fix the system. Simplify the processes. The headcount you thought you needed never materializes.

---

I have a rule: before I consider whether a person could help, I ask whether better tooling or a better process would solve the same problem.

Nine times out of ten, it would.

The tenth time, it's worth asking: is this actually a problem I need to solve, or is it a symptom of a larger problem I'm not seeing?

I run this as a literal exercise. When I feel the urge to hire, I write out the actual problem I'm trying to solve. Not "I need a developer" but "I need X feature built and I don't have time to build it." Then I ask: is this feature actually the constraint on the business right now? If yes, is hiring a developer the fastest path to getting it built? Or could I use AI-assisted coding tools and build it myself in a weekend? Or could I descope the feature and solve the underlying need a different way?

Most of the time, the exercise kills the impulse. The "I need to hire someone for this" feeling dissolves when you look at the actual bottleneck clearly. Hiring is often the first idea, not the best one.

Some companies need to hire. If you're building a product that requires human judgment at scale — consulting, therapy, sales of complex products, physical services — you need people. AI can assist, but it can't be the product.

But if you're building software, digital content, or data products? The assumption that growth requires proportional headcount is a legacy belief from a world where that was true. It was true when the tools were worse. It was true when automation required custom engineering. It was true before AI agents.

It's not true anymore.

---

Here's the test I use before any hire, real or hypothetical.

**The $200K Question:** If I had this person's salary to spend on tooling, automation, or product improvement instead, what could I build? Would that $200K in product improvement generate more value than the person would?

Most of the time, yes. But the question is worth sitting with. Because the math is more uneven than most founders realize.

A $200K hire costs more than $200K. Add benefits, payroll taxes, recruiting fees, onboarding time, equipment, software licenses. Call it $260K to $280K all-in. That's in the first year alone. In year two, the real cost includes management overhead. Your time. Every hour you spend in 1:1s, on performance reviews, answering questions, resolving confusion. If you're an early-stage founder, your time is the most expensive thing in the company. A new hire doesn't just consume cash. They consume you.

Now look at what $200K in tooling, infrastructure, or product improvement buys.

A properly designed automation stack at my scale costs under $1K a month. That's $12K a year. For $200K, you could build out a serious automation stack and still have six figures left over to improve the product itself. The thing that actually drives growth.

But the deeper advantage is compounding. The salary you pay this year is the same salary you pay next year, plus raises. The automation you build this year gets cheaper, faster, and smarter next year. Every major model provider has cut prices aggressively over the last two years. The systems I built in 2023 do more for less money in 2026.

People have fixed hourly output. Good tooling doesn't sleep. It doesn't have bad weeks. It doesn't disengage because someone else got promoted. It doesn't take two weeks to get up to speed on a new project. It doesn't give notice three weeks before your product launch.

---

There's also something worth naming about the asymmetry of the decision.

When you hire someone, you're making a one-way bet with a very long tail. Salary is a fixed cost that recurs every month. Management overhead compounds as the team grows. Culture forms around the people you've hired, which means your options narrow over time based on choices you made early. A hire you make in month six can still be shaping your culture and your cost structure in year four.

Tooling doesn't work that way. You can turn it off. You can swap it out. You can scale it up and down based on actual need. The cost tracks the usage. The usage tracks the value. That flexibility is worth something real, especially in the early stages when you don't know what you'll need a year from now.

The founder who hires to feel less anxious trades the anxiety for a different kind of fragility. The founder who delays a hire and builds better systems keeps their options open. Optionality is underrated. It doesn't show up on a pitch deck. But it's one of the most valuable assets a small company has.

---

The startup ecosystem has convinced founders that the path to success runs through headcount. Investors ask "what's your hiring plan?" as though more people is an obvious sign of ambition. Press covers "startup raises $X, plans to double team" as though doubling the team is the thing worth writing about.

It's not. The thing worth writing about is whether you're solving the problem.

Think about what it means structurally when a solo operator competes against a funded ten-person team. The funded team has meetings about priorities. The solo operator has priorities. The funded team has to align stakeholders before shipping. The solo operator ships. The funded team has to explain a strategic pivot to twelve people and then manage the emotional fallout. The solo operator pivots.

Speed is underrated as a competitive advantage. Not speed in a hustle-culture sense. Speed in the literal sense: the time from "I should change this" to "the change is live." Large teams have built-in drag on that timeline. Every coordination layer is a tax on speed.

There's also the question of what "growth" actually means when you optimize for it early. Hiring a growth team before you have strong product-market fit means you're systematically getting better at acquiring customers for a product that isn't fully working yet. You're pouring water faster into the leaky bucket. The growth metrics go up. The retention metrics don't move. And now you have a team you can't easily unwind, running experiments on a product you still haven't fixed.

The constraint of doing it yourself forces clarity. If you're the only person running an experiment, you learn from it immediately, directly. There's no game of telephone between the data and the decision-maker. You are both. You iterate faster because the loop is shorter.

---

You can always hire later. You can't easily un-hire.

You can't easily unwind the culture, the processes, the communication overhead, the management layers that accumulate once you start building a team. Each hire increases the complexity of every subsequent hire. The organization becomes load-bearing. People become load-bearing. Changing course gets harder with every quarter.

Stay small longer than feels comfortable. The leverage you're protecting is more valuable than the help you think you need.

Most early hires are a hedge against discomfort. Not a genuine unlock for the business. Learn to sit with the discomfort instead. That's where the real problem-solving happens.

The loneliness hire, the credibility hire, the hope hire, the anxiety hire. They all share a structure. You feel something uncomfortable. The hire makes the feeling go away. The problem stays.

Fix the problem. Hold the feeling. Stay lean.
