# Chapter 1: The Origin Story

Seven years ago, I worked for the city.

Government job. Union contract. Benefits, pension, the whole deal. The kind of job your parents tell you to be grateful for. And I was — for about six months.

Then I started counting.

Seven hours. That was my workday. Not eight. Seven. The union had negotiated it down, and nobody questioned it. Show up at 8, leave at 3, take an hour for lunch. Six hours of actual availability, maybe four hours of real work on a busy day. Most days, less.

I'd finish my tasks by noon and spend the afternoon looking busy. Alt-tabbing between spreadsheets when someone walked by. Taking long walks to the printer. Having conversations about conversations we'd already had.

The work wasn't hard. The work wasn't even bad. The work just... wasn't enough.

---

Here's what nobody tells you about government work: the ceiling isn't your salary. The ceiling is your output. There's a maximum amount of impact you're allowed to have, and it's enforced not by policy but by culture. Moving fast makes people uncomfortable. Suggesting improvements gets you labeled as "not a team player." Finishing early means you get assigned to help someone who's behind — which teaches you to never finish early.

I watched smart, capable people slow themselves down to match the pace of the institution. And the institution's pace was glacial.

Not because the people were lazy. Because the system was designed for stability, not speed. For consistency, not innovation. For process, not outcome.

And I realized: I was building someone else's thing, at someone else's pace, inside someone else's constraints.

Those aren't constraints that make you better. Those are constraints that make you smaller.

---

I didn't quit dramatically. No manifesto. No middle finger on the way out. I just started building on the side.

First it was freelance work. Web projects, mostly. Small businesses that needed something built and couldn't afford an agency. I was cheap and fast. That was my entire value proposition.

One of those early clients was a guy named Dave. He ran a chain of three dry cleaners in the suburbs. Needed a booking system. Simple enough. I quoted him $3,200, he paid half up front, and I built the thing in about two weeks.

Then the scope started moving.

He wanted a loyalty points system. Fine. Then a text reminder integration. Okay. Then a report that pulled data from all three locations and emailed it to him every Monday morning. Sure. Then he wanted the whole thing redesigned because his wife thought the blue was wrong.

I'd done all this extra work on a fixed quote because I kept thinking: this is almost done, just one more thing, don't lose the client. By the time I invoiced the final $1,600, I'd put in something like ninety hours. I made less than eighteen dollars an hour. The city job paid more.

But here's the real problem: I resented it. And that resentment was data.

I'd left one cage because the pace was too slow. And now I'd built myself a different cage, just for a guy with bad taste in website colors. The problem wasn't the client. The problem was the model. Trading time for money, one project at a time, with no leverage, no scale, and no ability to say no without losing the whole thing.

I finished Dave's project. He never left a review. I never took another client like that.

---

I shifted toward products instead of clients. Build once, sell many times. The logic was simple. The execution was not.

My first real product attempt was a scheduling tool for small gyms. I spent four months building it. I was proud of it. I showed it to maybe a dozen gym owners.

Not one of them would pay for it. A few said kind things. Most just didn't respond. One told me he already had something, and when I asked what, he said "a Google calendar." I'd spent four months building something that competed with a free calendar app, and I'd lost.

I rebuilt it. Repositioned it. Ran some ads. Made eleven sales in three months, then the sales stopped completely and I couldn't figure out why. I killed the product. Wrote off the time. Moved on.

That happened more than once. Different products, same pattern: build it, think it's good, find out the market doesn't agree.

There was a stretch of about eight months where I started wondering if I'd made a mistake. The city job was still there. Colleagues who'd stayed were getting steady raises. One got promoted. I was grinding on products that weren't working, doing occasional freelance work I hated, and waking up at 2am doing math in my head about how many months I had left before I needed to do something different.

I didn't go back. But I thought about it.

---

What changed wasn't a single breakthrough product. It was a tool.

I'd been spending real money on contractors to handle things I couldn't do myself. Mostly copywriting. I was decent at building, terrible at writing product descriptions, landing pages, email sequences. I had a contractor I used — paid her about $85 an hour, which felt painful every time I sent an invoice — and I'd use her maybe ten to fifteen hours a month. Call it a thousand dollars most months just for copy.

One afternoon in early 2023, I was sitting on a landing page rewrite I'd been putting off. My contractor was backed up. The deadline was a thing. I opened one of the early AI tools, typed in a rough description of what I needed, and hit enter.

It came back with something usable in about forty-five seconds.

Not perfect. But usable. Better than my first draft. I tweaked it for twenty minutes and published it.

I sat there for a second. Then I did the math.

Forty-five seconds versus two days of waiting, plus eighty-five dollars. I ran two more tasks through it that afternoon. Product descriptions I'd been stacking up. Each one took maybe three minutes of back-and-forth. The whole batch would have been four or five hours of contractor time.

I was paying maybe twenty dollars a month for the AI subscription at that point.

That was the moment. Not gradual. Not a slow realization. A very specific Tuesday afternoon in early 2023, sitting at my desk, doing arithmetic on a napkin and thinking: this changes everything.

I didn't fire my contractor that day. But I stopped sending her work. A month later I realized I hadn't needed to reach out at all.

---

Fast forward to today.

I run multiple companies. Different industries, different products, different revenue models. I have zero employees. Zero contractors. My entire "engineering team" costs less than a thousand dollars a month.

That's not a typo. Less than a thousand dollars a month.

The companies aren't side projects. They're real businesses with real users, real revenue, and real infrastructure. One is a SaaS analytics platform. One is a comparison site in a niche financial vertical. One is an AI-powered site builder for local businesses. I have others in various stages.

Each one would traditionally require a team. A developer or three. A designer. Someone handling customer support. Someone doing marketing. Someone managing the others.

To replicate what I run today with a traditional headcount, you'd spend something like $2.3 million a year. That's ten people, fully loaded, in a mid-tier market. Not San Francisco prices. Normal prices.

Instead, there's me. And the machines.

---

This book isn't a manifesto about how everyone should fire their team and go solo. Some companies need people. Some problems require human collaboration at scale.

But most don't. Most companies hire because that's what companies do. Because "we need to grow the team" feels like progress even when it isn't. Because founders mistake headcount for capability.

What I've learned is that the constraints you choose define the company you build. Choosing to stay small, to stay solo, to replace headcount with technology — that's not a limitation.

It's an advantage. The biggest one I've found.

---

Last week I was troubleshooting a backend issue at 11pm. Some data pipeline was misfiring on one of the platforms. I found the bug, fixed it, deployed the patch, and confirmed everything was running clean. Start to finish: about forty minutes.

Then I closed the laptop and watched TV.

No Slack message to send. No one to update in the morning. No postmortem meeting to schedule. No ticket to file. I fixed the thing and the thing stayed fixed and nobody needed to know about it because nobody else was depending on me to tell them.

That's the whole thing, right there. Not the forty-minute fix. The silence after it.

I've been chasing that silence for seven years. Turns out it was a design choice.
