# Chapter 1: The Origin Story

Seven years ago, I worked for the government.

Union contract. Benefits, pension, job security most people spend a career chasing. And I genuinely liked it. Not in a settling-for-less way — I actually liked the work, liked the people, and respected what we were doing.

But the job taught me something I didn't expect to learn there.

---

Working inside a union role inside a government institution means operating inside a very specific set of constraints.

You can't just hire someone when a problem gets too big. Headcount is a process that takes months. You can't bring in contractors for everything. You can't spin up a new team or throw money at a bottleneck. The budget is what it is. The timeline is what it is. The approval chain is the approval chain.

Most people experience those constraints as friction. Things to complain about. Reasons why the product is late or the feature is half-baked.

I experienced them as a forcing function.

When you can't add people, you get very creative about how the people you have work. When you can't extend the timeline, you get very clear about what matters and what doesn't. When every decision has to survive multiple layers of review, you learn to pre-solve objections before they surface. You build habits of efficiency because inefficiency has nowhere to hide.

We developed a product lifecycle process that was as lean as I've ever seen. Not because we were visionaries. Because we had no choice. The constraints forced us to be good.

That's the thing nobody tells you about working inside institutions with rigid constraints: if you're paying attention, they teach you more about lean execution than any startup accelerator ever will. You can't fake your way through it. You can't brute-force your way through it. You have to actually figure out how to do more with less.

I was paying attention.

---

I didn't leave because I was unhappy. I left because I needed more flexibility.

Life changes. Family, schedules, the way time fits together. The job I loved didn't fit the shape of the life I needed. So I left.

That's the whole story. No dramatic exit. No burning manifesto. No moment of revelation where I realized I was built for something bigger. I liked the work. I needed flexibility. I went and built something where I had it.

What I took with me — the thing I didn't realize I was carrying until much later — was that instinct for constraint-driven thinking. The reflex to ask: what can we accomplish without adding more? What can we automate instead of delegate? What does lean actually look like when you mean it?

It turns out that reflex is worth a lot outside of government.

---

Most companies don't think this way. Most companies hire because that's what companies do. Growth means headcount. Headcount means progress. More people means more capability — or so the logic goes.

That logic is wrong. Not universally. But most of the time, for most companies, in most situations: wrong.

What I've built since leaving that government job runs on a fraction of the people most comparable operations would require. Not because I'm cutting corners. Because I internalized — deeply, through years of working inside real constraints — that the default answer to most problems isn't "we need more people."

The default answer is: we need to think harder.

---

This book is the product of that thinking. It's what I learned from building lean — first by necessity, inside an institution that didn't give me any other option, and then by choice, building companies where I make the rules.

The constraint advantage isn't a hack. It's not a shortcut. It's a way of seeing problems that most companies never develop because they never have to.

They had the budget to hire their way out of it.

They didn't learn what I learned.

You're about to.
