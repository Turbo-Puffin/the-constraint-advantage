# Chapter 1: The Origin Story

Five years at an agency will teach you things no book can.

I started at Cantina as a Principal Consultant in user experience. Over five years, I worked my way up to VP of Delivery. In that time, I helped build products for Putnam Investments, McLaren, Walmart, Macy's, Granger Genetics, and more. Financial services firms. Race teams. Startups figuring out what they were building.

The work was real. The clients were real. The problems were real.

And I loved it. For a while.

---

The honest story of what happened at Cantina is this: I spent five years fighting for the right thing and compromising at the end. Every project. Every time.

Not because anyone was malicious. The clients weren't bad people. The firm wasn't a bad firm. But agency work has a structural problem nobody warns you about when you're climbing toward a VP title. The clock always wins.

On-time and under-budget delivery is the product. Not the actual product. Not the user experience. Not whether the thing you built actually works for the people using it. The delivery. The handoff. The final invoice.

I wrote about this in 2015 on Medium. Called it "burnout by a thousand cuts." That phrase was exactly right. It wasn't one moment. It was the accumulation of every meeting where the right answer got trimmed, every sprint where scope shrank to protect the timeline, every product that shipped just barely good enough. I kept fighting for the right thing. I kept compromising at the end.

That's not a complaint. That's just what agency work is. The constraint is the client relationship. The constraint is the contract. And those constraints, unlike the constraints I'd learn to love later, didn't make the work better. They just made it smaller.

By the end of 2015, I was done. Not angry. Not dramatic about it. Just done.

---

Then the City of Boston called.

I didn't go looking for it. It found me. It felt accidental at the time. It feels more like fate every year that passes.

The job was Lead Developer. The project was boston.gov. The environment was municipal government. Union contracts. Civil service rules. Fixed budgets. Approval chains. A pace that moves on its own schedule regardless of what anyone wants.

Most people hear "municipal government" and think: slow, frustrating, impossible to move.

I thought: at least it's honest.

---

The constraints at the City of Boston were real. Structural. Non-negotiable.

I couldn't add headcount because I wanted to. I couldn't spin up a new contractor because a problem was bigger than expected. The budget was the budget. The process was the process.

And something strange happened.

I loved it.

Not the limitations themselves. What I loved was what the limitations forced. When you can't buy your way out of a problem, you think your way out. When you can't add people, you design a better system. When every decision has to survive a review process, you stop making decisions you can't defend.

At Cantina, the constraint was the timeline and the client relationship. Those constraints compressed the work. At the City of Boston, the constraints were structural. Structural constraints don't compress the work. They clarify it.

What matters? What can we actually do? What does this city need?

Those questions have better answers inside a fixed budget. Flexibility invites scope creep. It invites gold-plating. It invites building things because you can instead of building things because people need them.

I once wrote a phrase that I still think is one of the truest things I've said about building public services: "developing with people, not for people." That wasn't a philosophy I brought to Boston. It was something Boston taught me.

When you work inside a democratic institution that serves real people with real needs, the feedback loop is different. There's no client to impress. There's no pitch deck to polish. There are constituents. People who need a form to work, a service to function, a website that doesn't require a law degree to navigate. You build for them. With them. Not for some abstract future user who lives in a persona document.

That clarity changed how I think about building things. Permanently.

---

In October 2016, boston.gov went open source.

That was under my watch as Lead Developer.

A government site going open source sounds like a technical event. It was a values statement. It meant other cities could fork the codebase, adapt it, improve it, build on what we built. It meant the work belonged to the public. It meant we were building something meant to be shared, not owned.

Other cities took notice. Some forked the repo. What we built at the City of Boston became a model. Not because we had unlimited resources. Because we had clear purpose inside real constraints.

That's the constraint advantage at city scale.

A team that couldn't hire freely, couldn't blow the budget, couldn't spend six months in design workshops. A team that had to decide what mattered and build that thing and ship it. A team that operated inside the most visible, most accountable kind of public accountability there is.

That team built something the rest of the country wanted to replicate.

---

I left Boston when I needed flexibility. Life changed. Schedules changed. The job I loved didn't fit the shape of the life I needed, and I cared too much about both to force the fit.

I want to be clear about this. I didn't leave because I was burned out. I didn't leave because I was frustrated with the institution. I left because I needed something the role couldn't give me, and that was okay.

What I took with me was everything.

---

I started Hondo Inc. Consulting work. Clients like Bose, MTA, the State of Connecticut, Chase. The same constraint-driven thinking I'd developed at Cantina and Boston, applied to new problems.

Then I co-founded Rudder with Megan Leary-Crist. A different kind of agency. One where the thesis wasn't just about delivery. It was about transformation. Public sector digital work. The kind of work where the goal isn't shipping a feature. It's changing how an institution serves the people who depend on it.

The clients changed. The work changed. The constraints changed shape each time.

But the thinking stayed the same.

---

Here is the thread I want you to see.

At Cantina, I learned that the wrong constraints make good work smaller. At the City of Boston, I learned that the right constraints make good work clearer. At Hondo Inc. and Rudder, I learned to choose my constraints instead of inheriting them.

When the MTA needed a ride-request tool for essential workers during COVID-19, we built it in six days. Not six months. Six days. Twenty thousand rides in the first four months. Workers could access it by phone or web. No app. No account. No friction.

That team didn't succeed because they had unlimited time or unlimited budget. They succeeded because the constraint was clear: workers needed a way to get to work, right now. No overbuilding. No features for later. Build the thing that solves the problem today.

When Marin County came to us behind schedule on a complex CMS migration, we proposed something simpler. Rebuilt in roughly thirty days. Twenty-one departments migrated over nine months. The constraint was: we don't have time for the complex thing. That constraint was right. The simpler approach was better.

These outcomes aren't coincidences. They're the thesis.

---

This book is about that thesis.

Constraints are not limitations. They are a forcing function for better thinking, better products, and better teams.

I've watched teams with unlimited resources build things that didn't matter. I've watched teams with almost nothing build things that lasted. The difference wasn't talent. It wasn't funding. It was clarity of purpose inside real limits.

The companies I run today operate under constraints I chose. No employees. No office. No venture funding. A hard cap on how much I spend on AI per month. These choices aren't compromises. They're competitive advantages. They force me to build systems instead of hiring around problems. They force me to think harder instead of spending more.

This book started at Cantina. It ran through a union job in Boston city government. It continued through solo consulting and a small agency that keeps choosing the harder, simpler path.

That path is not for everyone. But it works. And it works for a reason.

You're about to find out why.
