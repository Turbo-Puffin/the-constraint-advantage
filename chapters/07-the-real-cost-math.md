# Chapter 7: The Real Cost Math

Most founders do the revenue math obsessively. Projections. TAM. MRR growth curves. Spreadsheets that show you reaching $10M ARR by year three, assuming the hockey stick happens right on schedule.

Nobody does the cost math.

Not really. Not all the way down.

There's a reason for that. The revenue math is exciting. The cost math is sobering. Revenue math is about potential. Cost math is about obligation. You can spend hours building a model that shows you becoming rich, and that's a pleasant way to spend an afternoon. The cost math just sits there and stares back at you.

But obligation is the thing that will determine whether your business survives. Not potential. And the founders who understand their obligations clearly are the ones who make decisions that extend their runway instead of shortening it.

I want you to do the cost math right now. Not because the numbers are interesting. Because once you see the full picture, you cannot unsee it. And it will change every decision you make about how to build.

---

## What a Person Actually Costs

Ask a founder how much their team costs and they'll quote you payroll. "We have a $1.2M payroll." Like that's the number. Like that's what they're spending.

Salary is roughly 65% of what an employee actually costs. The rest hides in line items that don't feel like headcount costs until you add them up.

Here's the full anatomy of a 10-person startup.

---

**Salaries.**

This is the team you picture when you think about what you're building:

- 1 CEO/Founder: $130K (below market, because you're "committed")
- 1 CTO: $180K
- 2 Senior Engineers: $160K each = $320K
- 1 Junior Engineer: $90K
- 1 Designer: $120K
- 1 DevOps/Infra: $140K
- 2 Customer Support: $55K each = $110K
- 1 Marketing: $110K

**Subtotal, salary: $1,200,000**

Ten people. One million two hundred thousand dollars before you've bought a single laptop or paid a single tax.

---

**Benefits.**

Here's where it starts to get interesting.

The US employer benefits burden runs 20-30% of salary. Every person you hire comes with a shadow cost that doesn't show up in the offer letter. You're paying it regardless.

- Health insurance: ~$12,000/year per employee = $120,000
- Payroll taxes (FICA, FUTA, SUTA): ~8% = $96,000
- 401K match (3%): $36,000
- Workers comp, unemployment: ~$15,000

**Subtotal, benefits: ~$267,000**

That's $267,000 you spend to keep ten people healthy and legal. You don't decide whether to pay it. You just pay it.

---

**Equipment and tools.**

Every person needs a machine. Every machine breaks. Every role comes with software it can't function without.

- Laptops ($2,500 each, replace every 3 years): $8,333/year
- Software licenses (Figma, GitHub, Linear, Slack, Notion, etc.): $30,000/year
- Equipment for remote workers (monitors, peripherals): $3,000

**Subtotal, equipment/tools: ~$41,000**

Forty-one thousand dollars to make sure everyone has working tools. You will spend this money whether the tools are used well or not.

---

**Office space.**

If you're in a real city, you're paying real rent. And you're paying it every month whether the team is productive or not, whether the business is growing or not, whether anyone is actually in the office or not.

- Desks in a co-working space: $800/desk/month x 10 = $96,000/year
- Or: leased office space at $50/sqft x 2,000 sqft = $100,000/year

**Subtotal, space: ~$96,000-100,000**

Eight thousand dollars a month. Gone before a single line of code ships.

---

**HR and recruiting.**

This one stings. Not because the number is the biggest, but because of what it represents.

Think about what $116,000 in recruiting costs looks like in practice. It looks like months of job postings. Resume screeners. Phone screens with candidates who disappear. Hiring managers pulling two-hour interview blocks out of their week, four candidates at a time, for six weeks. Offer letters that get declined. Counteroffers. Finally landing someone and watching them quit in month four because a bigger company threw more money at them.

Then doing it again.

Recruiting fees run 15-20% of first-year salary per hire. If you hired half your team this year, that's $90,000 to $120,000 just in recruiter fees before you count the HR software, the legal costs, the compliance overhead.

- Recruiting fees (15-20% of first-year salary per hire, half the team hired this year): $90,000-$120,000
- HR software (Gusto, Rippling, etc.): $6,000/year
- Employer of record services, legal, compliance: $10,000/year

**Subtotal, HR: ~$116,000**

This is the cost of finding and keeping people. It never goes to zero. The moment you become a company with employees, this clock starts.

---

**Management overhead.**

This one is almost entirely invisible, which is why it's the most insidious.

Your CTO costs $180,000 per year. What does a CTO do? They're supposed to build the technical foundation of the company. Set architecture. Make hard calls about systems. Stay ahead of what the engineering team needs.

But your CTO is also a manager. And managers spend 30-50% of their time on people work. That means 15 hours every week doing one-on-ones, dealing with team conflict, writing performance reviews, figuring out who needs more challenge and who's about to burn out.

That's roughly $85,000 per year of a $180K salary going to coordination. Not output. Not product. Not competitive advantage. Coordination.

And that's just the CTO. Every lead on your team is doing the same thing at some fraction of their time. The management tax is real, it's large, and it scales linearly with headcount.

**Subtotal, management overhead: ~$85,000+**

You are paying $85,000 per year for meetings about people problems. That number will grow as the team grows.

---

**The full picture.**

| Category | Annual Cost |
|---|---|
| Salaries | $1,200,000 |
| Benefits | $267,000 |
| Equipment & tools | $41,000 |
| Office space | $96,000 |
| HR & recruiting | $116,000 |
| Management overhead | $85,000 |
| **Total** | **$1,805,000** |

Round up for the inevitable surprises, and you're at **$1.8-2.3 million per year.**

Before you've written a line of code. Before you've acquired a customer. Before you've made a single dollar.

## What $1.8 Million a Year Actually Feels Like

Let me make this concrete. Not abstract.

$1,805,000 per year is $150,416 per month. That's the monthly payroll meeting. The one where you stare at the number and calculate runway. If you raised $2 million, you have 13 months before the lights go out. Thirteen months to prove the thing works. Thirteen months to find product-market fit, build traction, and raise again before you have to let ten people go.

That math lives in your chest. Every founder I know who's run a team carries it around. The number behind the number. The real number. Not "how are we growing?" but "how many months until we have to have the hard conversations?"

The payroll meeting happens whether the month was good or bad. You hit a big customer. Great. Still owe $150K on the first. You had a brutal month. Churn is up, a major deal fell through, the product has a critical bug. Still owe $150K on the first. The number doesn't care about your circumstances.

And the team doesn't know what you know. They're working. They're building. Some of them are pretty happy, actually. They trust you to have a plan. That trust is one of the most fragile and important things you will ever hold. When the money gets tight, you'll spend more energy managing the communication around it than you will managing the actual problem.

Every hire is a bet. Not a small one. The fully-loaded cost of a single senior engineer, hired today, is somewhere around $250,000 per year when you run all the numbers. You are betting $250,000 that this person will generate more than $250,000 in value, that you can measure whether they're doing it, that they'll stay long enough to compound, and that the company will survive long enough for it to matter.

Most founders make that bet ten times without doing the math.

And most of those bets include a recruiting process that cost months of management time, an onboarding period where the new hire is at maybe 40% productivity, a ramp where you're not sure if they're going to work out, a moment around month six where you know one way or the other, and then either a long productive run or a painful separation process. The separation process, by the way, has its own costs. Severance. Legal review. The morale hit on the remaining team. The recruiting cycle starting over.

Meanwhile, the burn clock runs every day. Weekends. Holidays. Days when the team is in a planning meeting and nobody ships anything. Days when someone's sick. Days when there's a conflict that eats a week of everyone's emotional energy. The meter runs regardless.

There's also the cognitive load. When you're responsible for ten people's livelihoods, they live in your head in a way that has nothing to do with the work. You notice when someone seems off. You wonder if the quiet one is disengaged or just focused. You carry every interpersonal friction, every misalignment of expectations, every piece of feedback you haven't found the right moment to deliver. That cognitive load is not captured in any spreadsheet. But it is very real, and it is very expensive, in the currency of your attention.

That's the invisible pressure of a team. It's not just the cost. It's the obligation. You owe these people salaries every two weeks. You owe them a company that's going somewhere. You owe them the honesty to tell them if that changes.

That weight is real. That weight costs you something. It doesn't show up in the spreadsheet.

## The $9,000 Alternative

Here's my actual monthly bill across all companies:

**Language model API costs: ~$400/month**
- Primary model (Claude): $280/month
- Secondary/cheaper models for high-volume tasks: $90/month
- Specialized models (image, audio): $30/month

**Infrastructure for agents (servers, not counting product infra): ~$150/month**
- Agent runtime server: $50/month
- Databases agents use: $40/month
- Storage, networking: $60/month

**Agent platform and tooling: ~$200/month**
- OpenClaw runtime: $79/month
- Workflow orchestration: $49/month
- Monitoring and logging: $40/month
- Misc. SaaS (no longer need per-seat pricing): $32/month

**Total AI operating cost: ~$750/month, or $9,000/year.**

Nine thousand dollars a year.

Read that again.

That number is almost offensive in its smallness compared to what came before it. $9,000 a year is a rounding error on a single engineer's salary. It's less than one month of office rent. It's half the cost of the software licenses alone. It's less than what a 10-person team spends on coffee and team lunches in a year.

And it is not aspirational. It is what I actually spend, across multiple operating companies, right now.

I remember the first month my AI bill crossed $500. I thought something had gone wrong. I checked the logs. Nope. That was just it running. Doing the work. Across all of it.

The moment you fully internalize the comparison, something shifts. $1,805,000 versus $9,000. The same category of work, two radically different cost structures, running simultaneously in the same economy. One of them scales with headcount. The other scales with tokens.

Here's what $9,000 per year buys in a typical week:

- 3-5 production deploys across 4 companies
- ~50 customer support emails triaged and responded to
- 30+ social media posts published across multiple brands
- Weekly analytics reports generated and distributed
- Database backups, monitoring, alerting
- Code reviews and automated refactors
- Invoice tracking and payment follow-up
- Content calendar management
- SEO performance monitoring

The equivalent human cost for that workload in the US market is roughly $400,000 to $600,000 per year in salary. That covers the roles that touch each of those areas. DevOps. Customer support. Marketing. Analytics. Finance ops.

I'm doing it for $9,000 per year in AI costs plus my own time on the 30% that genuinely needs me.

That's the miracle. And I don't use that word lightly.

## The Breakeven Math

Here's the number I want you to carry with you.

If my AI costs $9,000 per year and a comparable team costs $1,800,000 per year, I need to produce exactly 0.5% of what that team produces to break even on cost.

Not 50%. Not 5%. One half of one percent.

Think about what that means in practice. Imagine a 10-person team working full weeks, full quarters, for a full year. Now imagine taking 0.5% of their annual output. That's roughly two and a half days of work. Total. For the year.

I need to match two and a half days of a 10-person team's annual output to break even on cost efficiency.

Now think about what it means if I'm not just breaking even but actually competitive. If my agents handle 60 or 70 percent of what that team would handle, I'm not winning on cost efficiency by a little. I'm winning by a factor that doesn't have a comfortable name. The ratio is roughly 200 to 1.

That ratio is the point. Not that AI is magic. Not that you can replace every human with a chatbot. The point is that the cost floor has dropped so far that the strategic calculus has changed completely.

Here's the other side of the breakeven equation. If you're a solo operator competing against a funded 10-person team, they need to be 200 times more productive than you to justify their cost structure. Maybe they are. Maybe they've assembled ten exceptional people who work in perfect harmony and produce extraordinary output.

Most teams don't work that way. Most teams have meetings about meetings. Most teams have communication overhead that scales with headcount. Most teams have at least two people who are coasting and one who's actively disengaged. The research on team productivity is not flattering.

You don't need to beat them. You need to stay competitive enough that your cost advantage matters. And your cost advantage is extraordinary.

Here's one more frame on the same math. If you're a solo operator generating $300,000 in revenue per year, your profit margin with a $9,000 AI cost is radically different than your profit margin with a $1,800,000 team cost. The team needs $1.8M in revenue just to cover its own cost, before you count any of the actual operating expenses of the business. You need $9,000. The difference between those two numbers, that spread, is what makes it possible to build a profitable business from day one without raising capital, without diluting ownership, without betting other people's livelihoods on your hypothesis.

The math doesn't just give you a cost advantage. It gives you options. You can be patient. You can experiment cheaply. You can change direction without a conversation about severance. You can say no to investors because you don't need their money to survive. You can build the thing slowly and correctly instead of fast and desperate.

All of that comes from the math. From a $9,000 annual operating cost instead of $1,800,000. The constraint is the advantage.

## The Honest Caveat

A well-run team with great people can do things I genuinely can't do alone, even with agents. Speed at true scale. Human relationships with enterprise clients. Physical presence. Deep domain expertise across multiple specialties at the same time. If you're building something that genuinely requires those things right now, the math changes.

But honest assessment of what genuinely requires human scale is rare. Most founders hire before they've exhausted what's possible without hiring. The default is "we need more people." The question should be "what specifically requires a person, and have we tested whether an agent can do it first?"

I'm not arguing that teams are always wrong. I'm arguing that the cost comparison should be explicit before you build one.

For the majority of digital businesses at the seed to Series A stage, where you're still figuring out what you're building and why, the cost comparison is devastating. You're paying $1.8 million per year to run an experiment that a solo operator with agents can run for $9,000.

Before you hire, ask the question. Can an agent do this? Can the agent do 80% of this and I do the other 20%? If the answer is yes, you don't have a hiring decision. You have a tooling decision. Those are very different.

Most people don't ask the question. They assume the answer. They see what needs doing and they reach for the human-shaped solution because that's the one they've always used. The shape of the problem looks like a headcount problem. So they solve it with headcount.

The table above is the cost of that assumption. $1,805,000 per year. Before a dollar of revenue.

Do the math. All of it. Then decide.
