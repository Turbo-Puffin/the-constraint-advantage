# Chapter 8: Things AI Can't Do (Yet)

I want to earn your trust by being honest about the limits.

Anyone who tells you AI agents can do everything is either selling you something or hasn't actually tried to run a business with them. There are real gaps. Significant ones. Knowing where those gaps are isn't pessimism. It's how you build systems that actually hold up.

This matters especially because the gap between what AI can do and what people believe AI can do is wide, and it runs in both directions. Some people underestimate what AI can handle and spend human time on things that could be automated. Other people overestimate it and trust AI output in places where that trust is not yet earned. Both errors are expensive.

So here it is: what AI genuinely can't do well. Yet.

---

## 1. Build Real Relationships

Sales is the most important skill in business, and the most irreplaceable one.

Not "send emails and book calls." The actual human art of understanding what someone needs, why they need it, what their politics are, what they're afraid of, what they're trying to prove. And then building trust across months and years until you're the person they call first.

AI can draft the cold email. It can research the prospect. It can generate objection-handling scripts and follow-up sequences. But it cannot be in the room when the deal is being made. It cannot feel the hesitation in someone's voice and know to stop talking. It cannot do the dinner where nothing business gets discussed and everything business gets decided.

The deals I've seen fall apart didn't fall apart because of pricing. They fell apart because the buyer didn't trust the person selling. And trust is not built through email sequences. It's built through accumulated experience. Through showing up when you said you would. Through being honest when honesty was inconvenient. Through knowing when to push and when to back off. None of that is transferable to an AI system right now.

For B2C products at scale, this matters less. For enterprise sales, for high-touch services, for anything where the relationship is the product, you still need humans.

The relationship is not a deliverable AI can produce. It's an experience two people have over time. That distinction matters.

---

## 2. Make Novel Strategic Decisions

AI is extraordinarily good at reasoning within established frameworks. It's surprisingly bad at deciding that the framework is wrong.

"Given these assumptions, what's the best pricing strategy?" AI handles this well. It can synthesize competitive data, consider positioning, model revenue scenarios. Give it a clear frame and it will work inside that frame with precision.

"Are we building the right product for the right market?" This is where it falls apart. Answering that question requires reconsidering the assumptions that everything else is built on. AI systems optimize within existing frames. They don't challenge the frame. That's not a flaw in a specific model. It's a structural bias in how these systems work.

The decisions that change the direction of a company — the pivot, the product line you kill, the market you enter when everyone thinks you're crazy, the technology bet that looks insane today — require genuine strategic creativity. The ability to look at the evidence and believe something different from what the evidence seems to say. That's still a human capability.

This is also where AI can mislead you in a dangerous way. If you ask it "should I pivot?" it will give you a thoughtful answer synthesized from everything it knows about pivots. That answer will be coherent, well-structured, and often beside the point. Because the right answer depends on things the AI doesn't know and can't fully weigh: the specific texture of your market, your personal runway, whether you still believe in the original thesis, what your gut has been telling you for three months. It will give you a framework when what you need is a decision.

Use AI for analysis. Make the consequential decisions yourself.

---

## 3. Exercise Taste

Taste is the judgment that something is good before you have data to prove it.

Good product taste. Design taste. Writing taste. The sense that this copy is off even though you can't explain exactly why. The intuition that this feature will resonate even though the market research doesn't support it. The feeling that this color is wrong.

AI can produce work that meets specifications. It can generate competent design, readable copy, functional product flows. It can learn your preferences through feedback and improve. It can get very close.

But taste — the thing that separates good from memorable — is still human. The founders who build products people love aren't just good at execution. They have strong opinions about what the right experience feels like. That opinion cannot yet be fully delegated.

Here's where it gets uncomfortable: most people overestimate their taste.

They think they have strong intuitions when they have weak preferences. They confuse "I like this" with "I know why this works." They mistake familiarity for quality. They mistake novelty for distinction.

If your taste is genuinely sharp, you know it because people seek out your opinions and you're right often enough to have a track record. If your taste is undeveloped, the honest move is to lean on data, build feedback loops, and treat AI output as a starting point for real user testing rather than a finished product.

Most people are somewhere in the middle. They have taste in one or two domains and thin preferences everywhere else. Knowing which is which is the hard part. Be honest about where you actually have strong judgment and where you're guessing.

---

## 4. Navigate Truly Ambiguous Judgment Calls

When the situation is genuinely unprecedented and the stakes are high, AI becomes less reliable.

"Should I accept this acquisition offer?" No. That involves your life goals, your risk tolerance, your read on the acquiring team's culture, your attachment to the thing you built, your financial situation, your sense of what you still want to prove. AI can model scenarios. It can list trade-offs. It cannot make the call.

"Is this the right time to end a contractor relationship?" Same issue. It involves relationships, timing, reputational considerations, your gut on whether they can turn it around, what message it sends. AI can list pros and cons. It cannot weight them for your specific situation.

The closer a decision is to irreversible, high stakes, and uniquely human, the more you want a human making it, with AI as a research assistant.

---

## 5. Do Novel Physical or Sensory Work

Obvious, but worth stating. AI agents can't taste your product, walk your retail store, hear the tone of a live customer conversation, or assess whether the hardware prototype feels right in the hand.

This also extends to anything that requires real-time human presence and judgment in ambiguous physical situations. A sales rep who walks a manufacturing floor and notices that the operations manager is uncomfortable, and reads that and adjusts. A customer success manager who sits across from a client and understands from body language that the relationship is in trouble before the client says anything. A founder who shows up in person and communicates commitment in a way no email or video call can replicate.

If your business has a physical component that requires sensory judgment, you need humans for that component. AI can help with everything around it.

---

## The Honest Problem With AI Output

Here's the thing I want to say clearly, because not enough people say it.

AI doesn't know when it's wrong.

It produces output with consistent confidence regardless of whether the answer is reliable or fabricated. Ask it to summarize a document and it does it well. Ask it to cite a specific statistic and it may invent one that sounds plausible. Ask it to analyze a situation with incomplete information and it will fill the gaps with plausible-sounding reasoning that isn't grounded in anything real.

The failure mode is not "AI refuses to answer." The failure mode is "AI answers confidently and incorrectly." And confident incorrect output can do more damage than no output at all, because you trust it.

This is called hallucination in the technical literature. I call it the thing that will burn you if you stop paying attention.

The practical fix is to treat AI output as a first draft, not a final answer. On anything consequential, verify. Spot-check. Build review steps into your workflows where the stakes are high. Over time you'll develop an instinct for where your specific systems are reliable and where they need a human check. Trust that instinct.

Never use AI output for anything financial, legal, or medical without independent verification. This isn't a limitation you can engineer around with better prompts. It's a structural property of how these systems work right now.

---

## Where This Leaves You

The honest picture: AI agents today handle roughly 70% of what a small business needs done, at dramatically lower cost and higher consistency than humans.

The remaining 30% — strategic judgment, relationship-building, taste, irreversible decisions, physical presence — still needs you.

That's not a consolation prize. It's the actual point.

Think about what the 70% actually looks like. It's the reporting. The scheduling. The first drafts. The monitoring. The customer support triage. The invoicing follow-up. The social posting. The data entry. The code review passes. The status updates. Every repetitive task that filled your day and made you feel productive while actually keeping you from the work that matters.

That 70% was not the work. It was the maintenance layer around the work. It masqueraded as work because it had to get done. But if you're honest, you know the difference between the day you spent clearing the queue and the day you made something. The 70% was the queue. AI runs the queue.

What AI has done is give you the day. Every day. The one where you do the 30% that only you can do, without the tax of the 70% that was never yours to do in the first place.

That's not a trade-off. That's a gift.

---

## The Split Is Moving

The 70/30 ratio isn't fixed. It's a snapshot taken today.

Every six months, the 70 gets bigger. Things AI couldn't do a year ago, it does routinely today. Things it struggles with today, it will handle competently by next year. The capability gap on relationship-building is narrowing. On strategic reasoning, it's improving. On taste, there are already cases where AI judgment is indistinguishable from expert human judgment on specific tasks.

Think about what has moved in the last two years alone. Document analysis that used to require a paralegal. Customer support that used to require a trained team. Code that used to require a senior engineer to write and a junior to review. Financial analysis that used to require an analyst. First-draft content that used to require a junior copywriter. These are not experiments anymore. They are production systems running in real businesses.

The direction is clear even if the pace is uncertain. The 30% will shrink. Some things that require human judgment today will not require it in three years. New capabilities will arrive that nobody is predicting right now, just as nobody three years ago predicted where we'd be today.

This matters enormously for how you build. If you design your systems around today's AI limitations, you're optimizing for constraints that will disappear. The founders who will compound their advantage over the next five years are the ones building habits and workflows that can absorb new capabilities as they arrive, rather than treating AI as a fixed tool with a fixed feature set.

Think of it like building on a platform that keeps adding features. Every quarter, something becomes easy that was hard before. If your systems are flexible enough to use the new capability, you get the upgrade automatically. If your systems are rigid, you have to rebuild. Flexible beats rigid. Adaptive beats static.

When something new becomes possible, the attentive founders integrate it in a week. Their competitors take two years to notice it and another year to act on it.

That lag is the advantage. It's not technical. It's attentional. You stay close to what's moving and you move with it.

I'm not saying AI will replace everything. I don't know that. Nobody does. Anyone who claims certainty about the five-year horizon is guessing with confidence, which is a different thing than knowing.

What I do know: the capabilities are increasing faster than most people are adjusting for. The gap between founders who are building with current AI and founders who are waiting to see how it plays out is already large. In two years, it will be larger.

Build for a moving target. Assume the constraints of today won't be the constraints of tomorrow. And stay close enough to the edge to know when yesterday's constraint disappears.
