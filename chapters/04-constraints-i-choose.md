# Chapter 4: Constraints I Choose

At some point the narrative shifted.

For the first couple of years of running solo, I described my situation apologetically. "It's just me right now." "I'm bootstrapped so I don't have a team yet." "I'm planning to hire when the revenue is there." All of it was true. None of it was the real story.

The real story is that I stopped wanting to hire before I was able to hire.

That's the shift nobody talks about. Not "I can't afford a team" but "I've thought about it carefully and a team would make me slower, more expensive, and harder to maneuver." The constraint stopped being external. I started choosing it.

---

There's a concept in architecture called constraint-driven design. The best bridges aren't built despite their constraints. They're built because of them. The span you need to cross, the load you need to carry, the materials you have available. These constraints don't limit the design. They generate the design. Remove the constraints and you don't get a better bridge. You get a blob of indecision.

When a constraint disappears, through funding, through growth, through the general abundance of having resources, most companies don't use that freedom to build something better. They use it to build something bigger. A company in the shape of every other company.

I watched this happen from inside the agency world. Clients with unlimited budget didn't build better products. They built more complicated products. They added features nobody asked for. They extended timelines because the constraint of the deadline had been replaced by the constraint of the next phase.

The constraint keeps you honest. Remove it, and you drift.

---

When I was the Lead Developer at the City of Boston, the constraints were the job. Union rules. Civil service. Fixed budgets. Approval chains. The public accountability that comes with building something for a city.

I didn't choose those constraints. They were the environment.

But here's what I learned: operating inside real constraints for long enough changes how you think. You stop looking for the escape hatch. You start asking better questions.

What can we actually accomplish with what we have? What is the minimum thing that solves the real problem? What are we building because we should, versus what are we building because we can?

Those questions don't come naturally in an environment of abundance. They come from having to answer to a fixed budget and a public that needs your site to actually work.

When I left government and started building on my own terms, I kept asking those questions. Not because I had to. Because they're better questions.

I chose the constraints. And the constraints I chose are tighter than the ones most people would accept.

---

My constraints are:

**No employees. Ever.**

**No office.** If I'm paying rent for a space that exists to house people, something went wrong.

**No venture funding.** I own everything or I own nothing.

**No meetings.** Every decision that requires a meeting is a decision that should be made differently.

**AI cost cap.** Less than $1,000 per month, across all companies. If I'm spending more, I'm either building the wrong thing or using AI inefficiently.

Some of these I've never broken. Some I've tested and come back to. The point isn't that these are the right constraints for everyone. The point is that having explicit constraints — ones you chose, that you can articulate and defend — forces rigor that growth and abundance don't.

---

"Less than $1,000 a month in AI" is the constraint that sharpens everything else.

When you have a budget, you allocate. When you allocate, you prioritize. When you prioritize, you understand your actual business better.

A company with unlimited AI spend gets sloppy. Spinning up agents for every half-baked idea. Running expensive models on tasks that a cheap model handles just fine. Using AI to feel busy rather than to accomplish something specific.

A company with a tight AI budget asks: what does this machine actually need to do? Then it designs the machine precisely. No fat. No waste. Every dollar pointed at something that produces value.

My monthly AI bill is predictable because I know exactly what each agent does, why it exists, and what it would cost to not have it. Most founders can't tell you what their team is doing in that level of detail. I can tell you what my AI is doing task by task.

That clarity isn't a side effect of the constraint. It is the constraint.

---

The counter-argument I hear most: "You're leaving money on the table by not scaling."

Maybe. Probably. I'm sure there are opportunities I've missed because I didn't have three salespeople or a growth team or a VP of partnerships.

But here's what I also know. Companies that scale too fast die of complexity more often than they die of missed opportunity. The startup graveyard is full of companies that raised too much, hired too many, and collapsed under the weight of their own overhead before the product was ready.

I'd rather be a small, profitable, low-overhead company that's still here in ten years than a well-funded, 50-person operation that ran out of runway because the costs scaled faster than the revenue.

Constraints aren't a consolation prize for not raising money. They're a survival mechanism. They're a decision-making framework. They're the thing that keeps you from building a company that needs a Director of Operations to manage the person who schedules the meeting that updates the spreadsheet tracking the progress of the thing you actually care about.

---

There's one more constraint I haven't mentioned yet, because it's the hardest one to talk about: ego.

---

The first time someone asked me how many people were on my team, I felt it in my chest before I answered.

It was a potential client. Early meeting. He was sizing me up the way people do when they're deciding whether to trust you with something that matters. "So how big is your operation?"

I told him the truth. "It's just me, plus a system of AI agents that handles most of the operational work."

There was a pause. The kind of pause that does a lot of work. He said "interesting" and we moved on. I got the contract. But I noticed the pause. I noticed it because I'd already given that same pause, internally, every time someone asked me that question.

*It's just me.* The word "just" is doing a lot of damage in that sentence.

---

Founders get their identity wrapped up in headcount the way people get it wrapped up in job titles. "We're a 12-person team" sounds like an accomplishment. "It's just me" sounds like an apology. We absorb this from the culture around us without ever deciding whether we believe it.

You see it at conferences. At dinner parties. At the awkward post-event networking where everyone is quietly ranking each other. Someone asks what you do, you say you run a few companies, they ask how many people, you say one — and you watch their face reclassify you in real time. Not a founder. A freelancer. Not scaling. Still figuring it out.

The dinner party answer is the hardest part. Not the building. Not the uncertainty. The thirty seconds where you either apologize for your org chart or you don't.

For a long time, I apologized. Not explicitly. Just in the framing. "It's just me right now." The "right now" was doing all the work — implying this was temporary, that real growth was coming, that I was moving toward something that would look more recognizable. More legitimate.

I don't say it that way anymore.

---

The shift didn't come from a book or a mindset coach. It came from looking at the actual evidence.

Some of the best software ever built was built by embarrassingly small teams. The org chart is not the product. The org chart is the thing you build after you've built something worth having, usually because investors or board members or HR consultants convince you that you need one.

I spent a couple of years optimizing for the wrong audience's validation. Building toward a version of the company that would look impressive to people who would never be my customers. The headcount was evidence for a case I didn't actually need to make.

---

Letting go of that required grieving something real.

Not the ambition. Not the drive. Not the desire to build something that matters, that got bigger, if anything. What I had to grieve was a particular image of what success looked like. The conference stage. The "we're hiring" tweet. The photo of the team at the offsite. The sense that those things were evidence of having made it.

They're not evidence of anything except that you have a team. And a team is not the point.

The point is: does the work matter? Does it work? Does it last?

I can answer yes to all three while sitting alone at a desk with a few hundred dollars a month in AI costs and customers who pay me reliably and no one to manage and no one to let go and no one's salary depending on a quarter that went sideways.

That's not failure. That's the whole game.

---

There's a harder version of this that I'll say plainly.

At the City of Boston, I operated inside constraints I didn't choose. The budget was the budget. The process was the process. I couldn't hire my way out of a problem. I couldn't extend the timeline because the politics were hard.

And we shipped a better product because of it.

Now I choose constraints that feel the same. They're tighter than they need to be by most people's standards. They force me to be creative instead of expensive. They force me to build systems instead of adding people. They force me to stay close to the work instead of managing layers between me and the work.

The constraints I choose are a commitment to that same quality of thinking. Not because I have to. Because I've seen what it produces.

Choose your constraints before the world chooses them for you. Because the ones you choose will make you sharper. The ones the world chooses will just make you smaller.
